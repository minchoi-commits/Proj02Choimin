Project 1 – Text Data: Social Media and Mental Health

Motivation & Research Question

Social media platforms contain large amounts of text that reflect users’ feelings and stress. Understanding textual patterns related to mental health can help identify risk factors at a population level.

Research Question: What textual features in social media posts are associated with negative emotional or mental-health-related content?

Dataset & Preprocessing

  • Collected text posts from a public dataset (e.g., social media or mental-health-related text).
  • Cleaned text by lowercasing, removing punctuation, and deleting stopwords.
  • Applied tokenization and calculated basic statistics such as word frequency and sentence length.

Methods

  • Computed sentiment scores for each post and extracted keywords related to stress, anxiety, or depression.
  • Grouped posts into negative vs. non-negative categories using sentiment scores or keyword rules.
  • Visualized distributions of sentiment and frequent keywords using bar charts and simple plots.

Key Results

  • Negative posts contained higher frequencies of mental-health-related keywords than non-negative posts.
  • Certain words (e.g., “tired”, “alone”, “anxious”) appeared much more often in negative posts.
  • Text-based indicators can highlight emotional states but do not directly represent clinical diagnoses.

Discussion & Limitations

  • Keyword- and sentiment-based methods are simple and can miss sarcasm, slang, and cultural context.
  • The dataset and labels may contain noise, and results cannot be generalized to all users or platforms.

Future Work & Impact

  • Combine basic text features with more advanced NLP models to capture subtle emotional cues.
  • Explore temporal patterns (posting time, frequency) to better understand changes in emotional states.
  • Insights may support large-scale mental health awareness but should not be used as diagnostic tools.

Project 2 – Image Data: Visual Emotional Tone of Memes

Motivation & Research Question

Memes are a popular way to express emotions on social media, including self-deprecating jokes and negative humor. These visual posts may reflect underlying stress or psychological struggles at a population level.

Research Question: Are there systematic differences in visual tone (brightness, saturation, colorfulness) between positive, neutral, and negative memes?

Dataset & Cleaning

  • Used the 6992 Meme Images Dataset with Labels from Kaggle.
  • Each image has a sentiment label (very_positive, positive, neutral, negative, very_negative).
  • Merged labels into three classes: positive, neutral, negative and removed invalid or missing entries.
  • Matched each label with its image file path and skipped unreadable or missing images.

Methods

  • Loaded meme images and converted them to HSV color space using Python and OpenCV.
  • Extracted three global visual features:
    • Brightness – mean of the Value (V) channel
    • Saturation – mean of the Saturation (S) channel
    • Colorfulness – numeric score based on RGB channel differences
  • Built a feature dataset for a random sample of about 1500 memes.
  • Compared feature distributions using boxplots and a brightness–colorfulness scatter plot.

Key Results

  • Neutral and positive memes are only slightly brighter on average than negative memes.
  • Brightness, saturation, and colorfulness distributions overlap strongly across all three sentiments.
  • The brightness–colorfulness scatter plot shows a positive correlation, but sentiment classes are mixed.
  • Overall, simple global visual features do not clearly separate positive, neutral, and negative memes.

Discussion

  • Visual tone alone is not a strong marker of sentiment in this meme dataset.
  • Many memes express emotion mainly through text, irony, and cultural context rather than color.
  • Color-based features are better interpreted as a rough proxy for emotional tone, not as a diagnostic signal.

Limitations & Future Work

  • Limitations: used only simple global color features; ignored meme text and real mental health data.
  • Future work: combine image features with meme text in a multimodal model and link them to survey-based mental health indicators.
